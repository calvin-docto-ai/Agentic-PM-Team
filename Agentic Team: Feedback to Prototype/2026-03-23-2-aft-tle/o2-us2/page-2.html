What Makes a Good TLE Response?

Here are examples of strong responses and common pitfalls to avoid.

✓ Strong Response Example

Patient: 52-year-old male with controlled hypertension, now presenting with asymptomatic elevated LDL.

Good Response: "Based on his SCORE risk profile (low-intermediate risk), recommend initiating moderate-intensity statin (atorvastatin 40mg daily) with lifestyle modification emphasis. Baseline liver and muscle function assessment recommended before starting. Follow-up lipid panel in 6-8 weeks. Consider increasing intensity if LDL remains >70 mg/dL."

✓ Specific, actionable, time-bound, evidence-based

✗ Response to Avoid

Same Patient Scenario

Weak Response: "The patient should be treated with statins. It's standard of care. Talk to a cardiologist if needed."

✗ Vague, no dosing, no timeline, defers responsibility

Response Template

  1. Clinical Assessment: Summarize patient's key features and relevant context
  2. Recommendation: Specific medication or intervention with dose/duration
  3. Justification: Brief evidence-based reasoning
  4. Safety Considerations: Contraindications, monitoring, or baseline tests
  5. Follow-up: When to reassess and what to look for
